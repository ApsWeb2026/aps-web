APS distinguishes between what life is and how it can be identified.

A definition specifies the organisational condition that constitutes life. In APS, life is defined as viability-oriented organisation: the constraint-closed organisation through which a system sustains its own persistence. This definition establishes what must be true of any living system, regardless of how it is observed or measured.

Diagnostics, by contrast, provide operational criteria for detecting life in practice. These include observable signatures such as self-maintenance, adaptive regulation, and responsiveness to perturbation. Such indicators are not themselves the definition of life, but evidence that the underlying organisation is present.

This distinction resolves a common source of confusion in biology. Lists of traits or behaviours are often treated as definitions of life, but they function only as diagnostic proxies. A system may display some diagnostic features without being fully alive, while a living system may lack certain expected traits under particular conditions.

APS therefore separates definition from detection. The definition establishes the organisational principle of life, while diagnostics identify where that principle is realised.

**In brief**  
Life is viability-oriented organisation; diagnostics identify systems that exhibit this organisation.
