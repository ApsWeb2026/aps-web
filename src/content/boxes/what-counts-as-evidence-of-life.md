Evidence of life consists in detecting the presence of viability-oriented organisation in operation. In APS, life is defined as the constraint-closed organisation through which a system sustains its own persistence. Evidence therefore lies not in isolated features, but in patterns of activity that indicate this organisation is present.

Observable indicators of life include self-maintenance, regulation of internal conditions, and adaptive responsiveness to environmental perturbation. These are not defining properties, but expressions of an underlying organisation that actively preserves its own viability.

No single trait is sufficient as evidence of life. Instead, evidence emerges from the coordinated interaction of multiple processes that together indicate a system is maintaining itself over time. The relevant question is not whether a system exhibits a particular feature, but whether its activity contributes to the continued persistence of its organisation.

This perspective distinguishes evidence from definition. Diagnostics identify candidate systems by detecting signatures consistent with viability-oriented organisation, while the definition specifies the organisational condition those signatures must reflect.

Evidence of life is therefore always indirect. It is inferred from the presence of organised, self-sustaining activity that cannot be reduced to externally imposed dynamics.

**In brief**  
Evidence of life is the detection of viability-oriented organisation in operation.
