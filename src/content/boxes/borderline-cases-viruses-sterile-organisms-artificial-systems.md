Borderline cases do not undermine the definition of life but clarify it. In APS, such systems are evaluated in terms of their capacity for viability-oriented organisation rather than by the presence or absence of particular traits.

Cases such as viruses, sterile organisms, and artificial systems are often taken to show that life cannot be clearly defined. In APS, these cases do not undermine the definition of life but test its explanatory adequacy.

Viruses, for example, lack the capacity to sustain their own persistence independently. Their activity depends on host systems that provide the organisational conditions required for replication. They therefore exhibit partial or dependent organisation rather than fully realised viability-oriented organisation.

Sterile organisms, by contrast, may lack reproductive capacity but still maintain themselves through internally organised processes. Their continued persistence is achieved through their own activity, and they therefore remain fully within the domain of life.

Artificial systems present a different challenge. Some exhibit complex behaviour or adaptive responses, but these are typically externally structured or maintained. Unless such systems establish constraint-closed organisation through which they sustain their own persistence, they do not meet the definition of life.

These cases demonstrate that life is not determined by the presence of particular traits, but by the organisation that integrates and sustains them. Borderline cases therefore clarify the boundary of life by revealing the conditions under which viability-oriented organisation is present, partial, or absent.

**In brief**  
Borderline cases are resolved by assessing the presence, absence, or dependence of viability-oriented organisation.
