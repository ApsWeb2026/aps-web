Normativity in biology concerns the distinction between what supports the continued persistence of a system and what undermines it. In APS, this distinction is not imposed externally but arises from the organisation of the system itself.

Living systems are viability-oriented: their activity is organised toward sustaining their own persistence. This organisation establishes an intrinsic asymmetry between states and processes that contribute to continued viability and those that lead toward breakdown.

Normativity therefore emerges from biological agency. Because a living system must actively maintain the conditions of its own existence, differences in internal and external conditions are not neutral. They are structured in terms of their consequences for persistence.

This form of normativity does not require representation, intention, or conscious evaluation. It is enacted through the system’s ongoing regulation of its own organisation. What “matters” to the system is determined by its viability.

Normativity provides the basis for understanding function, regulation, error, and adaptation. These are not merely descriptions of behaviour, but expressions of an underlying organisation in which some outcomes count as success and others as failure relative to the persistence of the system.
