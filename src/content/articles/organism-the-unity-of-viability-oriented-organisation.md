## The Problem of the Organism

The concept of the organism has long occupied a central but unstable position in biology. In classical traditions, organisms were treated as unified living beings—integrated wholes whose parts functioned together to sustain life. With the rise of mechanistic and gene-centred biology, this unity was progressively reinterpreted: organisms became vehicles, containers, or temporary assemblies of more fundamental components.

This shift introduced a tension. Biological explanation continued to rely on organisms—development, physiology, behaviour, and evolution all presuppose them—yet their ontological status became unclear. Are organisms real units, or merely convenient abstractions?

APS resolves this tension by reframing the organism not as an object, but as an organisation.

## The Organism as Organisation

In APS, the organism is a dynamically maintained, constraint-closed organisation through which viability-oriented activity is sustained across time.

An organism is not defined by:
- a fixed boundary  
- a list of parts  
- a genetic program  

It is defined by the ongoing integration of processes that maintain the conditions of its continued existence.

This shifts the explanatory focus:

- from structure → to organisation  
- from components → to integration  
- from static identity → to maintained continuity  

The organism is therefore not something that *is*, but something that *persists*.

## Agency, Normativity, and Persistence

The organism is the minimal unity in which three fundamental dimensions of life coincide:

- **Agency** — the ongoing activity through which the system sustains itself  
- **Normativity** — the viability-relative distinction between what supports and undermines persistence  
- **Persistence** — the maintained continuity of organisation across time  

These are not separate properties layered onto a system. They are aspects of a single organisational condition.

To be an organism is to be a system in which:

- activity is oriented toward viability  
- differences matter relative to that viability  
- organisation is maintained through time  

This unity cannot be reduced to molecular mechanisms, behavioural outputs, or evolutionary histories alone.

## Constraint Closure and Organisational Unity

APS grounds the organism in **constraint-closed organisation**.

Constraint closure does not merely stabilise a system. It establishes a regime in which processes collectively sustain the conditions that enable their own continuation.

Within such organisation:

- processes regulate one another  
- dependencies are internally organised  
- viability is actively maintained  

The organism is therefore not a passive result of interacting parts. It is an active organisation that continuously reconstitutes itself.

## The Organism–Environment Relation

In mechanistic accounts, the organism and environment are treated as separate domains: the organism acts, the environment provides conditions.

APS rejects this separation.

The organism–environment relation is constitutive:

- organisms do not exist independently of their environments  
- environments are not external backdrops but structured fields of affordances  
- viability is achieved through ongoing coupling  

The organism is therefore defined not in isolation, but through its maintained relation to the conditions that sustain it.

## Against Hierarchy and Reduction

Traditional biology often places organisms within hierarchical levels—between cells and populations. APS rejects this framing.

The organism is not a level. It is a **mode of organisation**.

This distinction is critical:

- organisms are not reducible to molecular processes  
- nor are they merely aggregates within larger systems  
- they are loci of integrated, viability-oriented organisation  

This reframing allows APS to avoid both reductionism and hierarchical reification.

## Organisms and Evolution

Evolutionary theory presupposes organisms, even when it attempts to explain them away.

Processes such as variation, inheritance, and selection require:

- entities that persist across time  
- systems that reproduce organisation  
- units in which viability can succeed or fail  

In APS, organisms are not secondary outcomes of evolution. They are the **conditions under which evolution becomes possible**.

Natural selection operates on differences in the persistence of organised systems. Without organisms, there is no substrate for evolutionary dynamics.

## The Organism Reframed

The organism is not a container of life. It is the organisation through which life is enacted.

It is not defined by what it is made of, but by what it does:
maintaining the conditions of its own continued existence.

**Key Point.** The organism is the minimal unity of agency, normativity, and persistence—a dynamically maintained organisation through which life sustains itself.
