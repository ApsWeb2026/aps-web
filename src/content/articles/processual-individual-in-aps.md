## From individual to continuity

A biological individual in APS is a unit of viability-oriented, constraint-closed organisation. However, living systems do not exist as static units. They persist through time by continuously maintaining and reorganising their own conditions of existence.

A **processual individual** is a biological individual understood in this temporal dimension: as a system whose identity is maintained through ongoing activity rather than fixed structure.

## Identity without static substance

Traditional accounts often treat identity as the persistence of the same substance or structure over time. In biological systems, this view is inadequate.

Cells divide, components are replaced, and internal organisation is continually reconfigured. Despite this, the system persists.

In APS, identity is grounded not in material continuity but in the **continuity of organisation**. A processual individual maintains its identity by sustaining a coherent regime of viability-oriented organisation across time.

## Continuity through organisation

The continuity of a processual individual depends on:

- the ongoing maintenance of constraint-closed organisation  
- the integration of processes that sustain viability  
- the capacity to reorganise in response to perturbation  

This continuity is dynamic. It involves change, turnover, and transformation, yet remains recognisable as the persistence of the same organised system.

Processual individuality therefore captures how living systems **remain themselves by changing**.

## Processual individuality and persistence

Persistence in APS is not the maintenance of a fixed state but the maintenance of a viable trajectory.

Processual individuals embody this principle. They persist by continuously adjusting their organisation while preserving the conditions required for viability.

This allows APS to account for identity across time without invoking static essences or immutable boundaries.

## Distinguishing process from classification

It is essential to distinguish **processual individuality** from **classification categories**.

- Processual individuals are **organised systems persisting through time**  
- Species are **patterns of continuity across populations and generations**  
- Taxa are **classificatory groupings that track similarities and divergence**  

Species and taxa do not persist as individuals. They describe how processual individuals are related and transformed across time.

This distinction prevents the reification of classification categories as biological entities.

## Processual individuality across scale

Processual individuality is not tied to a single scale.

At smaller scales, it may correspond to cellular systems; at larger scales, to multicellular organisms or integrated collectives. What matters is whether the system sustains a coherent trajectory of viability-oriented organisation.

This reinforces the APS view that biological organisation is **multi-scale and continuous**, rather than hierarchically partitioned.

## Why processual individuality matters

Clarifying processual individuality has several important implications:

- It explains how biological identity is preserved despite continuous change  
- It grounds persistence in organisation rather than substance  
- It supports a non-essentialist account of species and taxa  
- It integrates individuation with evolutionary transformation  

Without this concept, biological systems risk being misinterpreted as static entities rather than dynamic processes.

## Key Point

A processual individual in APS is a biological individual understood as a **continuously maintained trajectory of viability-oriented organisation**—preserving identity through ongoing change rather than fixed structure.
