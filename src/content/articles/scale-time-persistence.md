## 1. Introduction

The relationship between scale and temporal persistence is one of the foundational problems in process ontology. If all entities are processes — temporally extended activities rather than enduring substances — then what accounts for the apparent stability of the natural world? Why do cells, organisms, and ecosystems persist as recognisable entities despite continuous processual change?

This article argues that persistence is always scale-relative. What counts as "stable" or "persisting" depends on the temporal and spatial scale at which observation occurs. The APS framework formalises this insight through its treatment of scale as an irreducible dimension of [biological agency](/glossary/biological-agency/) and [process](/glossary/process/).

## 2. The Problem of Persistence

Process philosophy has long faced the challenge of accounting for stability. If everything is in flux, how do we explain the relative constancy of living systems? Traditional substance ontologies answer this question by positing enduring substrates that undergo change. Process ontologies must offer an alternative.

The APS framework's answer centres on self-maintaining organisation. A system persists not because some substrate endures, but because its constitutive processes are organised in a way that maintains the conditions for their own continuation ([Rescher, 1996](#ref-rescher-1996)).

## 3. Scale-Relative Stability

The key insight is that stability is always observed at a particular scale. At the molecular scale, a cell is in constant flux — proteins are synthesised and degraded, membranes are continuously remodelled. At the cellular scale, however, the cell persists as a recognisable entity with stable functional properties.

This is not merely an epistemic point about our observational limitations. The APS framework treats scale as an ontological dimension: the processes that constitute a system are genuinely different at different scales, and the stability conditions are scale-specific.

## 4. Implications for Flat Ontologies

Flat ontology approaches — which deny any privileged level of description — face a difficulty here. If all scales are equally real, then how do we account for the fact that stability and agency are manifested differently at different scales? The APS framework avoids this problem by treating scale as a structural condition for agency, not merely a perspective on it.

## 5. Conclusion

Scale is not an optional parameter in the analysis of persistence and agency. It is a constitutive dimension of both. The APS triad — Agency, Process, Scale — is irreducible: removing any one element undermines the explanatory power of the framework ([Thompson, 2007](#ref-thompson-2007)).
