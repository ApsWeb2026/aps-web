This article examines Active Inference and shows how APS reframes its central claims within a viability-oriented account of living systems.

## What Active Inference Explains

Active Inference, developed within the framework of the :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}, models biological and artificial systems as maintaining their organisation by minimising variational free energy. In this framework, systems act to reduce the difference between expected and actual sensory states.

This approach captures several important features of living systems:

- systems are active rather than passive  
- behaviour depends on system–environment coupling  
- internal organisation constrains possible states  
- adaptive regulation maintains coherence over time  

Active Inference therefore provides a powerful formal account of adaptive behaviour. It explains how systems can remain within a limited range of viable states by continuously adjusting their activity.

## Where APS and Active Inference Align

APS agrees with several core insights of Active Inference.

Living systems are not passive responders to external inputs. They actively regulate their own activity in relation to changing conditions. Their organisation constrains how they can act, and their continued existence depends on maintaining certain conditions over time.

APS also shares the rejection of simple input–output models. Behaviour is not the result of linear stimulus–response chains but of organised, system-level dynamics.

In this respect, Active Inference captures something real: systems are structured in ways that stabilise their trajectories through time.

## The Key Difference: Optimisation vs Persistence

Despite these similarities, APS and Active Inference diverge at a fundamental level.

Active Inference explains behaviour in terms of optimisation. Systems minimise free energy relative to a generative model. Their activity is understood as reducing uncertainty or prediction error.

APS argues that this does not explain what makes a system a living system.

Living systems are not defined by optimisation. They are defined by the fact that they must continue to exist. Their activity is organised around viability: the ongoing maintenance of the conditions required for their own persistence.

Optimisation describes how a system behaves under certain assumptions. Viability explains why those assumptions matter in the first place.

A system may minimise free energy, but this does not by itself explain why it must maintain its organisation, or how that organisation is generated and sustained.

## Models and Organisation

Active Inference often describes systems in terms of generative models that encode expectations about the world.

APS does not deny that some systems can exhibit model-like organisation. However, it rejects the idea that modelling is fundamental to life.

Living systems do not require internal representations or models in order to regulate their activity. Their organisation itself determines what counts as relevant and how activity is modulated.

Cognition, in APS, is not the use of a model to predict the world. It is the structured, constraint-sensitive organisation through which systems differentiate and regulate viability-relevant differences.

Model-based descriptions may capture certain patterns of behaviour, but they do not explain the underlying organisation that makes such behaviour possible.

## Free Energy and Viability

The free energy principle provides a formal description of how systems can remain within bounded states.

APS reframes this in terms of viability.

What matters for a living system is not the minimisation of an abstract quantity, but the maintenance of conditions that allow it to continue existing. These conditions are not externally specified or mathematically imposed. They are generated and sustained by the system’s own organisation.

Free energy minimisation can be interpreted as a description of certain stability conditions. But it does not replace the need to explain how systems establish and maintain those conditions in the first place.

## Machines, AI, and the Appearance of Cognition

Artificial systems built on Active Inference principles can exhibit highly adaptive and flexible behaviour. They may learn, optimise, and respond to changing environments in ways that resemble biological systems.

However, their organisation remains fundamentally different.

They do not sustain their own conditions of existence, do not operate through viability-oriented, constraint-closed organisation, and do not exist for themselves. Their objectives, architectures, and maintenance conditions are externally specified.

For this reason, such systems may display complex or intelligent behaviour, but they do not instantiate cognition in the APS sense. Cognition, in APS, is not defined by behavioural sophistication or predictive success, but by the organisation of activity within systems that regulate their own persistence.

## The APS Perspective: Organisation Before Optimisation

APS does not reject Active Inference.

It situates it.

From an APS perspective:

- Active Inference describes patterns of adaptive behaviour  
- it may provide useful formal tools for modelling such behaviour  
- but it presupposes systems that are already organised as living systems  

The primary explanatory task is therefore not to model optimisation, but to explain the organisation that makes persistence possible.

Life is not defined by the minimisation of free energy.

It is defined by the ongoing, viability-oriented organisation through which systems sustain themselves.

## Summary

Active Inference offers a powerful account of adaptive behaviour in terms of free energy minimisation and predictive regulation.

APS agrees that living systems are active, structured, and dynamically coupled to their environments.

However, APS argues that optimisation is not sufficient to explain life. Living systems are defined by viability-oriented, self-sustaining organisation, not by model-based inference or prediction error minimisation.

## Key Point

Active Inference describes how systems can stabilise their behaviour, but APS explains why living systems must persist: life is grounded in viability-oriented organisation, not in optimisation.
