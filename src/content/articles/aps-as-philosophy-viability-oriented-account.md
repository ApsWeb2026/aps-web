## 1. Biology and the Return of Philosophy

Biology is often presented as an empirical science concerned with the observation and explanation of living systems. Yet this presentation conceals a deeper truth: biological explanation is already structured by philosophical commitments. To identify a living system, to attribute a function, to describe a process as adaptive, or to distinguish cause from effect is never a purely observational act. Each involves assumptions about what exists, what persists, and what counts as an adequate explanation. These assumptions are rarely made explicit, but they shape the practice of biology at every level.
Theoretical biology emerges precisely at the point where these assumptions become visible. It asks not only what happens in biological systems, but how such happenings should be understood. It interrogates the conceptual structure of biological explanation itself. In doing so, it reveals that biology is not merely a collection of empirical findings but a discipline grounded in an implicit philosophy of life.
The Agency–Process–Scale (APS) framework enters at this point of tension. It does not introduce philosophy into biology from the outside. Rather, it reconstructs the conceptual structure that biological explanation already presupposes. In doing so, it transforms implicit assumptions into explicit commitments. Once articulated, these commitments can be examined, refined, or challenged. Biology thus becomes, in part, a philosophical enterprise—not by abandoning empirical practice, but by clarifying the conditions that make that practice meaningful.
What emerges from this clarification is not a replacement for biological explanation, but a reorientation of it. Explanation is no longer understood as the identification of isolated mechanisms alone, but as the articulation of organised relations that sustain and transform living systems over time. In this sense, APS does not merely contribute to theoretical biology; it reframes the conceptual conditions under which biological explanation itself becomes intelligible.

## 2. From Biological Framework to Ontological Proposal

APS is initially presented as a framework for understanding living systems. It emphasises that organisms are not static entities but ongoing processes, that their persistence depends on continuous activity, and that this activity is organised across interacting spatial and temporal scales. These claims resonate with contemporary developments in systems biology, developmental biology, and ecology, where organisation and interaction have increasingly replaced static structural description as the primary focus of explanation.
However, when these claims are brought together within a single explanatory framework, they begin to function as more than descriptive generalisations. They define the conditions under which something can count as living. To say that life is processual is to reject a substance-based ontology of biological entities. To say that it is viability-oriented is to introduce intrinsic normativity. To say that it is constraint-closed is to assert that living systems maintain the conditions of their own persistence. And to say that it is scale-integrated is to deny that explanation can be confined to any single domain or privileged level of analysis.
At this point, APS no longer operates solely as a descriptive or integrative framework. It begins to articulate the structural conditions under which biological explanation is possible. The transition is subtle but decisive: what begins as a way of organising biological knowledge becomes a specification of what biological reality must be like in order for that knowledge to cohere.
Taken together, these commitments amount to an ontological proposal. APS does not merely describe life; it specifies what life must be in order to be intelligible within biological science. It is therefore best understood as a candidate account of biological reality, derived from scientific practice rather than imposed upon it.
APS therefore occupies an intermediate ontological register. It is neither a purely metaphysical thesis nor a purely empirical generalisation. Rather, it functions as a scientifically anchored metaphysics: a framework whose ontological commitments are constrained by biological practice but not reducible to it. In this sense, APS does not merely redescribe biological findings but articulates the form of reality presupposed by those findings.

## 3. Explanatory Grammar and the Conditions of Biological Understanding

A defining feature of APS is its emphasis on explanatory grammar. Rather than beginning with a catalogue of entities, it begins with the conditions under which explanation is possible. This represents a significant shift in philosophical orientation. Instead of asking what exists independently of our explanatory practices, APS asks what must be presupposed for biological explanation to be coherent in the first place. The focus is not on the inventory of the world, but on the structure that makes that inventory intelligible as biological.
This shift does not deny the reality of biological entities. Cells, organisms, and ecosystems remain indispensable objects of scientific inquiry. What changes is how they are approached. They are no longer treated as primary explanatory units whose properties are to be analysed in isolation, but as organised systems whose intelligibility depends on relations that are not reducible to their components. Explanation, on this view, is not a process of decomposing reality into parts alone, but of identifying the organisational conditions under which those parts function as components of a living system.
In this sense, APS places itself in dialogue with a long philosophical tradition concerned with the conditions of intelligibility. In one respect, it echoes the Kantian project: to determine what must be true of experience—or in this case, of scientific explanation—for it to be possible at all. APS does not adopt Kant’s transcendental framework, nor does it claim that the conditions it identifies are imposed by the structure of cognition. Instead, it treats these conditions as emerging from the practice of biology itself. The constraints on explanation are not derived from the mind, but from the organisation of the phenomena being explained. What is at stake is not the possibility of experience in general, but the possibility of biological explanation in particular.
This reorientation has important consequences. Biological explanation is no longer understood as a neutral reflection of an independently structured world. It is a structured activity governed by conceptual constraints that reflect the organisation of living systems. To explain a biological phenomenon is not simply to identify its causes, but to situate it within a network of relations that make its persistence, variation, and transformation intelligible. Explanation becomes inseparable from the articulation of organisation.
Within this framework, agency, process, and scale are not independent ontological categories. They are analytic projections of a single organised reality. Biological agency names the capacity of a system to modulate its own activity in relation to its persistence. Process names the temporal continuity of that activity. Scale names its extension across spatial and temporal domains. These are not separate features of the world but different ways of articulating the same phenomenon: organised persistence. They do not divide reality into parts; they provide coordinated perspectives on a unified system of relations.
This triadic structure also functions as a constraint on explanation. Any adequate account of a biological system must be able to articulate how activity is regulated (agency), how it is sustained through time (process), and how it is distributed across spatial and temporal domains (scale). Explanations that omit one of these dimensions may be locally successful, but they remain incomplete. In this sense, explanatory grammar is not merely descriptive; it is normative in a methodological sense. It determines what counts as an adequate explanation within the domain of biology.
This perspective aligns APS with contemporary philosophy of science, particularly approaches that treat explanation as a practice rather than a mere description. On these accounts, explanations do not simply mirror reality; they organise it in ways that render phenomena intelligible. APS extends this insight by specifying the organisational conditions under which biological phenomena can be understood as living systems. It does not reduce explanation to linguistic or pragmatic considerations, but ties it directly to the structure of biological organisation.
APS can therefore be understood as providing a grammar of biological explanation—a structured set of conceptual relations that determines how biological systems can be meaningfully described and understood. This grammar does not replace empirical investigation, nor does it impose an external framework upon it. Rather, it makes explicit the constraints that biological explanation already operates under, and in doing so, provides a more coherent and integrated account of how life is to be understood.

## 4. Constraint Closure and the Structure of Organisation

The concept of constraint closure occupies a central position in APS. Biologically, it refers to the organisation of processes such that the constraints enabling those processes are themselves maintained by the system. Living systems do not simply operate within constraints; they regenerate them. This recursive organisation is what allows persistence to be sustained over time, not as static stability but as an ongoing achievement.
Constraint closure marks a decisive shift in how biological organisation is understood. In non-living systems, constraints may shape behaviour, but they are typically externally imposed or passively maintained. In living systems, by contrast, constraints are actively produced and sustained by the very processes they enable. The system does not merely depend on its conditions of operation; it participates in their continued existence. Persistence is therefore not the endurance of a structure, but the continuous regeneration of the conditions that make that structure possible.
This feature distinguishes living systems from other forms of dynamic organisation. A flame or a whirlpool may exhibit stability over time, but their persistence depends on a stable flow of external inputs. If those inputs cease, the system collapses without compensatory response. In contrast, a living system actively maintains the conditions of its own existence. It regulates exchanges with its environment, repairs internal disruptions, and reorganises its activity in response to changing conditions. It is not merely stabilised; it is self-stabilising in a way that is intrinsically tied to its continued viability.
The explanatory implications of this shift are significant. Mechanistic accounts describe how parts interact to produce outcomes, and they remain indispensable in biology. However, they often leave open the question of how those parts are maintained, repaired, and integrated over time. A mechanism may be fully specified in terms of its components and interactions, yet its continued operation presupposes a broader organisational context that is not itself explained by the mechanism alone. Constraint closure addresses this gap. It explains not only how processes occur, but how the conditions enabling those processes are continuously sustained.
In this sense, constraint closure can be understood as the organisational counterpart to explanatory grammar. Where explanatory grammar specifies the conceptual conditions under which biological systems can be made intelligible, constraint closure specifies the material conditions under which those systems can persist. The two are closely related. A system that does not maintain the constraints that enable its own activity cannot be coherently described as a biological individual within the APS framework. Organisation and intelligibility thus converge on the same structural requirement.
A concrete illustration can be found in metabolic self-maintenance in bacterial cells. Enzymatic networks generate metabolites that, in turn, stabilise the very enzymatic structures that produce them. Membrane transport maintains ionic gradients that enable the metabolic reactions that sustain membrane integrity. These processes are not independent; they form a network of reciprocal dependence in which each contributes to the conditions required by the others. This recursive organisation exemplifies constraint closure in practice and demonstrates how APS can be operationalised in empirical systems.
What is critical in such cases is not the presence of particular components, but the pattern of organisation that links them. Individual molecules are continually replaced, reactions fluctuate, and environmental conditions vary. Yet the system persists because the organisation of its processes maintains the constraints that sustain it. Persistence is therefore not identity through sameness, but identity through organised renewal.
In this way, constraint closure provides a unifying principle for understanding biological individuality, function, and adaptation. A biological individual is not defined by its material composition or spatial boundaries but by the closure of its organisational processes. Function is not merely a role played by a component within a system, but a contribution to the maintenance of that system’s constraint structure. Adaptation, in turn, can be understood as the reorganisation of constraint-closed processes in response to changing conditions, such that viability is preserved across time.
Constraint closure thus transforms how biological explanation is framed. It shifts the focus from isolated mechanisms to integrated organisation, from static structures to ongoing processes, and from externally defined stability to internally sustained persistence. It provides the structural basis for understanding how living systems exist, endure, and change—not as collections of parts, but as dynamically organised systems that continuously regenerate the conditions of their own existence.

## 5. Conceptual Closure and the Problem of Circularity

The strength of APS lies in its internal coherence. Its central concepts—agency, viability, function, adaptation, and evolution—are mutually reinforcing. Each is defined in relation to the others, forming a tightly integrated explanatory system. This interdependence gives the framework its unifying power. However, it also raises a philosophical question: does APS explain these concepts, or does it merely stabilise them within a closed network of definitions?
At issue is not simply whether the framework is circular, but what kind of circularity is involved. This distinction can be clarified by differentiating between vicious and virtuous circularity. Vicious circularity occurs when a concept is defined in terms of itself in a way that provides no explanatory gain, leaving the phenomenon no better understood than before. Virtuous circularity, by contrast, arises in systems where interdependence reflects genuine structure. In such cases, the mutual definition of concepts is not a logical flaw but a consequence of the organisation of the phenomena being described. The apparent circularity does not block explanation; it reveals the relational character of what is being explained.
APS appears to operate in this second mode. Its conceptual closure mirrors the organisational closure of the systems it describes. Just as biological systems maintain the constraints that enable their own persistence, APS maintains its conceptual structure through reciprocal definition. Agency is understood in terms of viability-oriented regulation; viability is understood in terms of persistence; persistence is understood in terms of organised process; and process is characterised by its contribution to viability. These relations do not form a simple definitional loop, but a network of interdependent concepts that jointly articulate the structure of living systems.
The crucial question is whether this interdependence is explanatory or merely terminological. If the concepts of APS simply redescribe one another without adding independent constraints on explanation, then the framework risks collapsing into circularity in the problematic sense. If, however, each concept introduces a distinct dimension of analysis—normative, temporal, organisational, or evolutionary—then their interdependence reflects the multi-dimensional structure of the phenomena themselves. On this reading, conceptual closure is not a weakness but a necessary feature of an adequate account of biological organisation.
This is where the analogy with constraint closure becomes more than illustrative. In biological systems, closure is not a matter of isolation but of organised interdependence: processes depend on one another in ways that collectively sustain the system. Similarly, in APS, conceptual closure does not isolate the framework from critique; it organises its core concepts into a mutually constraining system. Each concept limits and specifies the others, preventing arbitrary expansion while maintaining coherence. The framework is closed in the sense of being internally structured, not in the sense of being insulated from challenge.
To ensure that this closure remains explanatory rather than self-protective, APS must specify conditions under which its commitments could be revised or rejected. For example, if a system exhibited long-term persistence without regenerating its enabling constraints, the centrality of constraint closure would be undermined. Likewise, if viability-oriented regulation could be fully explained in purely mechanistic terms without reference to agency-like organisation, the explanatory role of agency would need to be reconsidered. These are not merely hypothetical possibilities; they define the conditions under which APS would need to change.
Articulating such counter-scenarios is not a concession but a methodological requirement. It demonstrates that the framework is open to empirical and conceptual challenge, and that its coherence is not maintained by excluding alternatives. In this way, APS distinguishes between structural circularity, which reflects the organisation of biological systems, and protective circularity, which would shield the framework from revision. The former is a source of explanatory strength; the latter would undermine it.
Conceptual closure, understood in this way, becomes a diagnostic feature of the framework. It indicates that APS is attempting to capture a domain in which explanation cannot be linear or unidirectional, but must instead account for systems whose defining characteristic is organised interdependence. The challenge is not to eliminate circularity, but to show that it corresponds to the structure of the phenomena themselves. Whether APS succeeds in this task remains an open question—but one that can only be addressed by engaging the framework on its own terms and testing its explanatory limits.

## 6. APS and the Refinement of Process Philosophy

APS aligns with the tradition of process philosophy in treating reality as dynamic and relational rather than static and substance-based. Within this tradition, what persists is not a fixed entity but a pattern of activity maintained through time. Stability is understood as a structured form of change, and identity is constituted through ongoing transformation rather than preserved despite it. In this respect, APS resonates with a long line of philosophical thought in which becoming is treated as more fundamental than being.
However, APS does not simply adopt this orientation; it refines it. Process philosophy, in its general form, often operates at a level of abstraction that leaves key distinctions underdetermined. A wide range of phenomena can be described as processes: flames, whirlpools, ecosystems, economies, and living organisms all exhibit dynamic organisation. Yet not all such systems are biological, and not all forms of persistence are equivalent. The claim that “everything is process” risks becoming too permissive to carry explanatory force unless it is supplemented by criteria that differentiate kinds of process.
It is at this point that APS introduces a decisive specification. Rather than treating process as a sufficient condition for life, it treats it as a necessary but incomplete one. What distinguishes biological systems is not that they are processes, but that they are organised in particular ways. Constraint closure identifies systems that maintain the conditions of their own persistence. Viability orientation introduces an intrinsic asymmetry between states that sustain the system and those that undermine it. Scale integration situates this organisation across interacting temporal and spatial domains, ensuring that persistence is not momentary but extended.
These features transform process ontology into a biologically grounded framework. They do not replace the insight that life is processual, but they specify the form that process must take to constitute living systems. Process, in APS, is not mere change or flow. It is organised activity structured in relation to the continued persistence of the system that enacts it.
This refinement has two important consequences. First, it stabilises process philosophy for biological use. Without such specification, process ontology risks collapsing into a descriptive generality that applies equally to living and non-living systems. APS restricts the domain of application by introducing organisational criteria that can, in principle, be investigated empirically. In this sense, it converts a broad metaphysical orientation into a framework capable of supporting scientific explanation.
Second, this specification introduces a productive tension. By grounding process in constraint closure, viability, and scale, APS gains precision but potentially limits its generality. If these conditions are required for a system to count as biological, then the applicability of APS beyond biology becomes a question rather than an assumption. The framework does not deny that non-biological systems exhibit complex dynamics, but it withholds the attribution of biological organisation unless these specific conditions are met.
In this way, APS can be understood as both continuous with and divergent from process philosophy. It affirms the central insight that reality, at least in the biological domain, is fundamentally dynamic and relational. At the same time, it resists the tendency to treat process as an undifferentiated category. By specifying the organisational structure of living systems, APS transforms process philosophy from a general metaphysical stance into a disciplined account of biological reality.
This transformation is not merely philosophical. It has direct implications for how biological systems are identified, analysed, and compared. To recognise a system as living is not simply to observe that it changes over time, but to determine whether its processes are organised in ways that sustain its own persistence. The question is no longer whether something is a process, but what kind of process it is—and whether its organisation satisfies the conditions required for life.

## 7. Normativity and the Naturalisation of Value

APS claims that living systems embody intrinsic normativity. Some states support persistence, while others undermine it. This asymmetry is not imposed from outside, nor does it depend on the presence of conscious evaluation. It arises from the organisation of the system itself. A living system does not merely undergo change; it operates under conditions in which some trajectories sustain its continued existence while others lead toward its degradation or collapse.
At first glance, this claim may appear straightforward. Biological systems are often described in terms of regulation, repair, and adaptation, all of which presuppose a distinction between conditions that are favourable and those that are not. However, the philosophical significance of this distinction is not immediately clear. It is one thing to observe that certain states contribute to persistence and others do not; it is another to claim that this difference constitutes genuine normativity.
This is where the distinction between descriptive asymmetry and evaluative force becomes crucial. APS identifies a real asymmetry in biological organisation: some states objectively contribute to persistence, while others undermine it. This asymmetry is not a matter of interpretation or projection; it is grounded in the causal organisation of the system. The question, however, is whether this asymmetry is sufficient to ground normativity in the stronger sense—whether it justifies the use of evaluative language such as “better,” “worse,” “success,” or “failure.”
By explicitly distinguishing these layers, APS avoids conflating functional description with evaluative commitment. It does not assume that any causal contribution to persistence automatically carries normative weight. Instead, it treats normativity as emerging from a specific organisational condition: the dependence of the system’s continued existence on the regulation of its own activity. Where persistence depends on ongoing modulation of conditions, differences begin to matter in a way that is not captured by neutral causal description alone. The system is organised such that some differences make a difference to its continued viability.

This perspective challenges traditional accounts of teleology in biology. Classical teleological explanations appeal to external purpose or design, while more recent naturalistic accounts often ground function in evolutionary history. The selected-effects approach, for example, defines the function of a trait in terms of the effects for which it was historically selected, while causal-role accounts define function in terms of a component’s contribution to a system’s operation. APS departs from both by locating evaluation in present-time organisation. A process is functional because it contributes to the ongoing maintenance of the system that enacts it, not because it was selected in the past or serves an externally defined goal.
This shift has important consequences. It brings normativity into the present tense of biological activity. A system does not need to represent its goals or interpret its environment in order for its behaviour to be described as normatively structured. It need only be organised such that its continued persistence depends on the regulation of its own processes. In this sense, normativity is not added to biological systems at higher levels of complexity; it is present wherever organised persistence is at stake.
At the same time, APS resists the temptation to overstate this claim. The existence of a viability-relevant asymmetry does not, by itself, resolve all questions about normativity. It remains open whether such asymmetry is sufficient to ground normativity in the full philosophical sense, or whether additional conceptual resources are required. For example, one might argue that genuine normativity requires not only differential consequences for persistence, but some form of justification, representation, or evaluative standpoint. APS does not attempt to resolve these debates in advance. Instead, it clarifies the conditions under which they arise.
In doing so, APS reframes the problem. Rather than asking how normativity can be introduced into a purely neutral world, it asks how a world containing organised, self-maintaining systems gives rise to distinctions that matter for those systems. The focus shifts from the origin of value in abstraction to the role of value in the organisation of life. Normativity becomes a feature to be explained in relation to persistence, not an external addition to it.
This reframing also connects back to the broader structure of the APS framework. The normativity associated with viability is not an isolated feature but is integrated with agency, process, and scale. Agency expresses the system’s capacity to modulate its activity in relation to what matters for its persistence. Process provides the temporal continuity within which such modulation occurs. Scale situates these dynamics across extended domains of interaction. Normativity, in this context, is not a separate layer but an aspect of organised activity itself.
Whether this account ultimately succeeds in grounding normativity remains an open question, and it is one of the most productive points of engagement with the framework. What APS offers is not a final resolution, but a reconfiguration of the problem—one that situates normativity within the dynamics of life rather than at its margins. In doing so, it opens a space for understanding value as intrinsic to the organisation of living systems, while remaining sufficiently constrained to invite critical scrutiny.

## 8. Empirical Tractability and the Status of APS

APS is unusual among philosophical frameworks in its explicit commitment to empirical tractability. It does not present itself as a purely interpretive or speculative account of life, but as a framework that can guide and be constrained by scientific investigation. This commitment is expressed in the development of diagnostic criteria, the identification of biosignatures, and the effort to operationalise core concepts such as constraint closure, viability, and agency in ways that can be applied to real systems.
In this respect, APS occupies a distinctive position. It does not merely interpret existing biological knowledge; it seeks to shape how that knowledge is generated. By specifying what counts as organised persistence, it provides a basis for identifying living systems, distinguishing degrees of biological organisation, and evaluating borderline cases. The framework therefore functions not only as a conceptual clarification, but as a methodological tool.
This orientation is particularly evident in the development of diagnostic approaches associated with APS. Rather than defining life in terms of a fixed list of properties, these approaches assess systems in terms of their capacity to sustain and regulate the conditions of their own persistence. Biosignatures, in this context, are not isolated markers but expressions of underlying organisational structure. They are indicators of processes that maintain constraint closure, modulate activity in relation to viability, and integrate across scales. The emphasis shifts from static traits to dynamic organisation.
However, this integration of conceptual structure and empirical application introduces a significant tension. The criteria used to identify and evaluate biological systems are derived from the theoretical commitments of APS itself. This raises the possibility that the framework may be, at least in part, self-confirming. If the diagnostics are defined in terms of the concepts they are meant to test, then the framework risks organising observations in ways that inevitably support its own assumptions.
The challenge, therefore, is not simply to operationalise APS, but to ensure that its operationalisation remains open to independent constraint. For the framework to function as more than an interpretive lens, it must be possible, in principle, for empirical findings to count against it. This requires that its concepts generate expectations that can be tested in contexts not fully determined by the framework itself.
One way to articulate this requirement is to distinguish between application and constraint. A framework can always be applied to a set of phenomena; the question is whether those phenomena can also constrain the framework. In the case of APS, this means asking whether systems can be identified whose behaviour challenges the necessity or sufficiency of its core concepts. The counter-scenarios outlined in the previous section—systems that persist without constraint closure, or that exhibit viability-oriented regulation without agency-like organisation—are not merely philosophical exercises. They define the empirical boundaries within which the framework must operate.
At the same time, the relationship between theory and observation in biology is never entirely external. All scientific frameworks shape the way phenomena are identified and interpreted. The goal is not to eliminate this interaction, but to manage it in a way that preserves the possibility of revision. APS acknowledges this by treating its concepts as both descriptive and normative: they guide explanation, but they also impose constraints on what counts as an adequate account.
If successful, APS would represent a rare integration of philosophical clarity and scientific applicability. It would provide a framework in which conceptual precision enhances, rather than replaces, empirical investigation. Its value would lie not only in the coherence of its internal structure, but in its capacity to generate new questions, refine existing methods, and identify previously unrecognised patterns of organisation.
If unsuccessful, however, APS risks functioning as an interpretive framework that organises data without being genuinely constrained by it. In that case, its concepts would remain internally coherent but externally underdetermined. The distinction between these outcomes depends not on the elegance of the framework alone, but on its capacity to engage with empirical systems in ways that expose it to potential revision.
Empirical tractability, in this sense, is not simply a feature of APS but a condition of its legitimacy. It is what allows the framework to move beyond philosophical articulation and participate in the ongoing development of biological science. Whether it succeeds in doing so remains an open question, but one that can only be answered through sustained interaction between conceptual analysis and empirical investigation.

## 9. Scope and Extension Beyond Biology

APS is grounded in biology, but its conceptual structure invites extension. The framework is built around organised persistence, constraint closure, and viability-oriented activity—features that appear most clearly in living systems, but that also resonate with patterns observed in other domains. This raises a natural question: to what extent can APS be extended beyond biology without losing its explanatory force?
One domain in which this question arises is cognition. APS proposes continuity between life and mind by treating cognition as a form of viability-oriented regulation rather than as a capacity dependent on representation or neural complexity alone. On this view, the processes that underlie metabolism, regulation, and adaptation already exhibit the basic structure of evaluation: they differentiate between conditions that sustain persistence and those that undermine it. Cognitive phenomena, in this sense, can be understood as elaborations of organisational capacities that are already present in biological systems. This suggests that APS may provide a unified framework for understanding both life and mind, without reducing one to the other.
A similar possibility arises in ecological and systems-level analysis. Ecosystems, social systems, and other large-scale organisations exhibit forms of distributed coordination and persistence that appear to echo the scale-integrated dynamics described by APS. Processes are not confined to individual units but extend across networks of interaction, and stability is achieved through ongoing adjustment rather than static equilibrium. These parallels invite the application of APS concepts beyond the level of individual organisms, raising questions about whether larger-scale systems might also exhibit forms of constraint closure or viability-oriented organisation.
The framework also opens questions in domains traditionally considered outside biology, such as ethics and epistemology. If viability-oriented organisation introduces a form of intrinsic normativity, then the distinction between descriptive and evaluative domains may need to be reconsidered. Values, on this view, are not imposed from outside the natural world but arise from the conditions under which systems persist. Similarly, knowledge can be understood not as a detached representation of reality, but as a process embedded within the organisation of living systems, shaped by the requirements of viability and adaptation.
These possibilities suggest that APS has the potential to function as more than a framework for biology. It may provide a conceptual bridge between biological organisation and broader questions about mind, value, and knowledge. However, this potential is accompanied by a significant risk. The strength of APS lies in its specification of biological organisation through constraint closure, viability orientation, and scale integration. If these concepts are extended too loosely, they may lose their discriminatory power.
This risk can be understood more precisely as a problem of conceptual dilution. If any sufficiently complex or stable system is described as exhibiting “process,” “organisation,” or even “persistence,” then the distinctive contribution of APS is lost. The framework risks collapsing into a general form of systems theory or process metaphysics, in which the same concepts apply equally to living and non-living systems without meaningful distinction. In such a case, APS would no longer function as a specification of biological reality, but as a broad descriptive language lacking clear criteria for application.
To avoid this outcome, extension must be disciplined by the same criteria that define the biological domain. Concepts such as constraint closure and viability orientation cannot be treated as loose analogies; they must be applied in ways that preserve their organisational meaning. The question is not whether non-biological systems exhibit complexity or persistence, but whether they exhibit the specific form of organised, self-maintaining activity that characterises living systems. Where these conditions are not met, the application of APS concepts must remain either provisional or explicitly metaphorical.
This does not mean that APS cannot be extended beyond biology. Rather, it means that such extension must be carefully justified. In some cases, it may reveal genuine continuities between biological and non-biological domains. In others, it may clarify the limits of those continuities. The framework thus provides not only a basis for extension, but also a means of evaluating its legitimacy.
The challenge, therefore, is not simply to determine whether APS can be generalised, but to identify the conditions under which generalisation remains meaningful. This involves distinguishing between structural continuity and superficial resemblance, between systems that share organisational principles and those that merely exhibit similar patterns of behaviour. APS does not resolve this issue in advance, but it provides the conceptual tools needed to address it.
In this way, the question of scope becomes an extension of the framework’s core concerns. Just as APS seeks to clarify what distinguishes living systems from non-living ones, it also provides a basis for evaluating how far those distinctions can be extended. Its proper domain may remain biological, but its implications reach beyond biology insofar as they illuminate the conditions under which organised systems persist, adapt, and acquire significance.

## 10. APS Among Contemporary Frameworks

APS occupies a distinctive position within contemporary theoretical and philosophical approaches to life. It does not emerge in isolation, nor does it seek to replace existing frameworks wholesale. Instead, it intersects with several major approaches while diverging from each in systematic ways. Its contribution lies not in rejecting these traditions, but in reorganising their central insights within a more explicitly articulated explanatory structure.
With enactivism, APS shares an emphasis on organism–environment coupling and the rejection of representational models of cognition. Both frameworks treat cognition as an active process grounded in the dynamics of living systems rather than as the manipulation of internal symbols. However, enactivism is primarily concerned with the nature of cognition and experience, and often takes the organism as its starting point. APS extends this orientation by situating cognition within a broader account of biological organisation. Rather than treating cognition as a distinctive domain requiring its own theoretical framework, APS treats it as a particular expression of viability-oriented regulation. In doing so, it shifts the explanatory priority from cognition to organisation, and from mind to life.
With autonomy theory, APS shares the concept of constraint closure and the idea that living systems are self-maintaining organisations. Autonomy theory has been instrumental in clarifying how systems can generate and sustain the conditions of their own existence, and APS builds directly on this insight. The divergence lies in integration and scope. APS incorporates constraint closure into a broader explanatory grammar that includes scale and normativity, thereby extending its explanatory reach. Where autonomy theory focuses on the internal organisation of self-maintaining systems, APS situates that organisation within a multi-scale framework and links it explicitly to questions of evaluation, persistence, and transformation.
With systems theory, APS shares a focus on organisation, interaction, and the dynamics of complex systems. Systems theory provides powerful tools for analysing networks of interaction and patterns of stability and change. However, it often operates at a level of generality that does not distinguish clearly between biological and non-biological systems. APS addresses this limitation by introducing specific organisational criteria—constraint closure, viability orientation, and scale integration—that differentiate living systems from other complex systems. In this sense, APS can be seen as a specification of systems theory for the biological domain, adding a layer of conceptual discipline to an otherwise highly general framework.
With mechanistic approaches, particularly those associated with the new mechanist tradition, APS shares a commitment to explaining biological phenomena in terms of organised processes rather than abstract laws. Mechanistic explanation has been highly successful in identifying the components and interactions that produce biological effects. APS does not reject this approach; instead, it situates it within a broader framework. Mechanisms explain how particular processes occur, but they do not, by themselves, explain how the systems in which those processes operate are maintained over time. APS extends mechanistic explanation by incorporating the conditions of persistence—how mechanisms are sustained, repaired, and integrated within constraint-closed organisation.
These comparisons reveal a common pattern. Each of the frameworks considered captures an important aspect of biological organisation: enactivism emphasises embodied interaction, autonomy theory clarifies self-maintenance, systems theory models complexity, and mechanistic approaches analyse organised processes. APS does not discard these insights; it brings them together within a single explanatory grammar that makes their relations explicit. At the same time, it introduces constraints that prevent any one of these perspectives from expanding beyond its domain of validity.
In this sense, APS is integrative but not eclectic. It does not simply combine elements from different frameworks, but reorganises them in a way that reflects the structure of the phenomena under investigation. The triadic framework of agency, process, and scale provides a common coordinate system within which different approaches can be located and evaluated. Agency captures the regulatory dimension emphasised in enactivism and autonomy theory. Process captures the temporal continuity central to process philosophy and mechanistic accounts. Scale captures the distributed organisation highlighted in systems theory. Together, these dimensions form a unified explanatory structure that is more than the sum of its parts.
At the same time, APS introduces a level of conceptual discipline that limits overextension. As discussed in the previous section, the framework resists the tendency to generalise its concepts beyond the conditions that define biological organisation. This distinguishes it from approaches that treat concepts such as “system,” “process,” or “interaction” as universally applicable without specification. APS insists that such concepts acquire explanatory force only when tied to the organisational conditions that sustain living systems.
The result is a framework that both integrates and constrains. It integrates by bringing together insights from multiple traditions into a coherent structure. It constrains by specifying the conditions under which those insights apply. In doing so, APS provides not just another perspective on life, but a way of organising perspectives—a meta-level contribution that clarifies how different approaches relate to one another and where their limits lie.
APS can therefore be understood not merely as one framework among others, but as an attempt to articulate the conceptual architecture within which contemporary biological explanation operates. Its success depends not on replacing existing theories, but on its ability to render them more coherent, more comparable, and more responsive to the organisational realities of living systems.

## 11. Conclusion

APS is best understood as a philosophically structured, empirically grounded attempt to articulate the nature of biological reality. It reframes life as constraint-closed, viability-oriented process extended across scale, and in doing so brings into focus the organisational conditions that underlie biological explanation. Rather than introducing a new set of entities or mechanisms, it clarifies how living systems must be understood if biological inquiry is to remain coherent.
This clarification operates at multiple levels. As an explanatory grammar, APS specifies the conditions under which biological phenomena can be made intelligible. As a theoretical framework, it integrates insights from across contemporary biology and philosophy into a unified structure. As a methodological proposal, it seeks to connect conceptual analysis with empirical investigation through diagnostics and operational criteria. In each of these roles, it aims not to replace existing approaches, but to render them more coherent, comparable, and constrained.
At the same time, APS remains an open project. Its central claims—regarding constraint closure, intrinsic normativity, and the organisation of persistence—are subject to both empirical and philosophical scrutiny. The framework must demonstrate not only that it can organise existing knowledge, but that it can be challenged, refined, and, where necessary, revised in light of new evidence and argument. Its coherence is therefore not a final achievement, but an ongoing condition of its development.
For this reason, APS does not offer a final answer to the question of what life is. Instead, it clarifies the conditions under which that question can be meaningfully asked and investigated. It reframes the problem from one of definition to one of organisation, from the identification of traits to the articulation of processes that sustain and transform living systems over time.
In doing so, APS contributes not only to biology but to philosophy. It provides a disciplined way of thinking about life that remains grounded in empirical practice while engaging directly with foundational questions about causation, organisation, and value. Whether it ultimately succeeds as a comprehensive account of biological reality remains to be determined. What it offers, at this stage, is a structured and testable way of approaching that question—one that invites both application and critique, and that situates the study of life within a broader inquiry into the nature of reality itself.
