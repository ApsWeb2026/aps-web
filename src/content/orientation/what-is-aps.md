## Process

APS treats processes as ontologically fundamental. Systems are not things that undergo processes; they are processes that give rise to relatively stable structures. This commitment follows the tradition of process philosophy and has consequences for how we understand persistence, identity, and change.

## Scale

Scale is not an optional parameter. The APS framework treats scale as a constitutive dimension of both agency and process. What counts as an agent, what counts as stable, and what counts as a process depends on the scale of observation. This is an ontological claim, not merely an epistemic one.

## The Core Structure of APS

APS is built on a small set of tightly integrated concepts that together define what life is and how it must be explained.

At its core, APS defines life as viability-oriented, constraint-closed organisation. Living systems are not identified by traits or components, but by the organised activity through which they sustain their own persistence.

This organisation is maintained through constraint closure: networks of mutually sustaining constraints that preserve the conditions required for continued existence. Because persistence depends on this organisation, not all states are equivalent. Some support viability while others undermine it. Within this organisation, biological agency emerges as the active regulation of conditions that sustain viability. Living systems do not merely persist; they actively modulate the organisation that makes persistence possible.

At the same time, this organisation is distributed across scale. Biological systems persist through the coordination of activity across spatial and temporal domains, rather than through hierarchical levels.
