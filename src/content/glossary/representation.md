## Conventional framing

Representation is typically understood as the internal depiction or encoding of external states of the world. In cognitive science and philosophy of mind, representations are often treated as mental or computational structures that stand in for features of the environment, enabling organisms to model, predict, or reason about their surroundings.

## APS reframing

In APS, representation is not a necessary condition for biological cognition. Basic organism–environment interactions do not depend on internal models or symbolic encodings, but on the direct modulation of activity relative to viability. Systems respond to conditions based on how those conditions affect their capacity to sustain themselves, not by constructing detached descriptions of the world.

Representation emerges only under more complex organisational conditions, where systems are able to regulate activity relative to states that are not immediately present. In such cases, internal structures may stabilise patterns of evaluation that guide action across temporal or contextual gaps. These structures function as representations insofar as they enable activity to be modulated relative to absent, anticipated, or counterfactual conditions.

This evaluative extension is grounded in biological normativity. The viability-oriented organisation of the system establishes the conditions under which activity succeeds or fails, and representational structures persist only insofar as they support this ongoing regulation. A representation is therefore “accurate” not by mirroring an external reality, but by contributing to the continued viability of the system that employs it.

Representation is therefore a derived and optional feature of cognition, not its foundation. Cognition begins with viability-based evaluation and only later, in some systems, acquires representational depth.

## Key Point

Representation is a derived form of cognition in which viability-based evaluation is stabilised and extended across absent or non-immediate conditions.
