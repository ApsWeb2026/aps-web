## Conventional framing
Purpose is traditionally associated with intention, design, or goal-directed behaviour. In biological contexts, this has led either to teleological explanations invoking ends as if they were intended, or to the rejection of purpose altogether in favour of mechanistic accounts.

## APS reframing
APS naturalises purpose by grounding it in viability-oriented organisation rather than intention or design. Purpose refers to the organisation of a system’s activity relative to the conditions required for its persistence.

Living systems continuously regulate their activity in ways that sustain their organisation. This activity is not random but organised relative to conditions that support or undermine continued existence. Purpose names this system-level organisation of activity.

Purpose is therefore inseparable from biological agency. Agency is the activity through which a system maintains its organisation; purpose is the organisation of that activity relative to viability conditions.

Purpose is a global concept: it describes how the activity of the system as a whole is organised. It does not specify the roles of individual components—that is the role of function—but the overall orientation of activity toward maintaining persistence.

By grounding purpose in constraint-closed, viability-oriented organisation, APS avoids both external teleology and reduction to mechanism. Purpose does not require foresight, design, or representation; it is the organisational condition under which activity is directed toward sustaining the system itself.

## Key Point
Purpose is the organisation of a system’s activity relative to the conditions required for its persistence.
