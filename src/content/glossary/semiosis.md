## Conventional framing

Semiosis is typically defined as the process by which signs are produced, transmitted, and interpreted. In classical semiotics (e.g. Charles Sanders Peirce), it involves a triadic relation between a sign, its object, and an interpretant. In biology, semiosis has often been extended to describe signalling, communication, or information processing within organisms.

## APS reframing

In APS, semiosis is not a symbolic or representational process but an organisational feature of living systems. It consists in the structuring of activity such that differences come to matter for viability-oriented regulation.

Living systems are normatively organised: some conditions contribute to their persistence, while others undermine it. Semiosis is the structuring of this asymmetry. It is the way in which differences are enacted as meaningful through their role in modulating activity.

A difference is meaningful for a system when it affects how the system regulates itself in relation to viability. This does not require internal representation, symbolic encoding, or interpretation. Meaning is not assigned or decoded, but realised in the system’s differential organisation of activity.

In this sense, semiosis is coextensive with biological normativity. It expresses the system’s sensitivity to the asymmetry between what sustains and what undermines its organisation. The “meaning” of a condition is therefore not an abstract content, but the way in which that condition enters into the regulation of activity.

Semiosis is a basal feature of biological organisation. Wherever systems differentially modulate their activity in relation to viability, semiosis is present. What varies across systems is the form, scope, and integration of this structuring.

Cognition is the organisation of semiosis. In more elaborated systems, the structuring of meaningful differences becomes increasingly integrated, temporally extended, and coordinated across the system as a whole. This allows present activity to be modulated in relation to conditions that are not currently realised, without requiring representation or explicit modelling.

Meaning, in this sense, is not representational content but the enactment of viability-relevant difference within organised activity.

## Key Point

Semiosis is the structuring of meaningful difference in living systems: it makes differences matter for viability and is organised, in more elaborated forms, as cognition.
