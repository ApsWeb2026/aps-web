## Conventional framing

Semiosis is typically defined as the process by which signs are produced, transmitted, and interpreted. In classical semiotics (e.g. Charles Sanders Peirce), it involves a triadic relation between a sign, its object, and an interpretant. In biology, semiosis has often been extended to describe signalling, communication, or information processing within organisms.

## APS reframing

In APS, semiosis is not a symbolic or representational process but an organisational feature of living systems. It consists in the structuring of activity such that differences in the environment come to matter for viability-oriented regulation.

Semiosis arises from evaluation. Because a living system differentially modulates its activity relative to conditions of persistence, some differences come to have consequences for its continued existence while others do not. Semiosis is the organisation of this differentiation: it is the process by which differences are enacted as meaningful through their role in modulating activity.

A difference is meaningful for a system when it affects how the system regulates itself in relation to viability. This does not require internal representation, symbolic encoding, or interpretation. Meaning is not assigned or decoded, but realised in the system’s differential response to conditions that bear on its persistence.

In this sense, semiosis is coextensive with biological normativity. It expresses the system’s sensitivity to the asymmetry between what sustains and what undermines its organisation. The “meaning” of a condition is therefore not an abstract content, but the way in which that condition enters into the modulation of activity.

Semiosis is a basal layer of biological organisation. All systems that exhibit evaluation also exhibit semiosis, insofar as their activity is modulated by differences that matter for viability. However, semiosis does not by itself constitute cognition. In many systems, meaningful differentiation remains tied to immediate conditions and local regulation.

Cognition arises when semiosis is organised across time. Where meaningful differences are integrated into temporally extended patterns of evaluation—such that present activity is structured in relation to conditions that are not currently realised—semiosis acquires counterfactual depth and becomes cognitive.

Meaning, in this sense, is not representational content but the enactment of viability-relevant difference within evaluative activity.

## Key Point

Semiosis is the organisation of meaningful difference in living systems: it makes differences matter for viability, and provides the basis from which cognition emerges.
